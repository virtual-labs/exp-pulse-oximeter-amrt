Key Principles

Beer-Lambert Law

The amount of light absorbed by a solution is proportional to the concentration of the absorbing substance and path length through it.

Dual Wavelength Detection

Red light (660 nm) and infrared light (940 nm) are used because oxyhaemoglobin and deoxyhaemoglobin absorb these wavelengths differently.

Photoplethysmography (PPG)

Volume changes in blood vessels caused by each heartbeat modulate the transmitted light signal, enabling both SpO₂ and pulse rate measurement.

Normal Ranges

Healthy SpO₂: 95–100%. Pulse rate: 60–100 bpm. Perfusion index > 1.0% indicates good circulation.

Pulse Oximeter Glossary

Pulse Oximeter

A non-invasive device that estimates blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) and pulse rate using light absorption through tissue.

SpO2 (Oxygen Saturation)

The percentage of hemoglobin carrying oxygen in arterial blood. Normal healthy readings are typically around 95-100%.

Sensor Alignment

How accurately the clip sensor is positioned on the fingertip. Better alignment improves signal quality and measurement reliability.

Infrared Light (940 nm)

One of the two wavelengths used in pulse oximetry. Oxygenated hemoglobin absorbs infrared light differently than deoxygenated hemoglobin.

Red Light (660 nm)

Used with infrared light to estimate oxygen saturation through differential absorption based on the Beer-Lambert principle.

Signal Verification

The step where the app confirms proper sensor placement and stable pulsatile waveform before monitoring starts.

Perfusion Index (PI)

A relative indicator of pulse strength at the sensor site. Low perfusion can reduce signal confidence.

Photoplethysmography (PPG)

An optical technique that detects blood volume changes in microvascular tissue, producing a pulsatile waveform for heart-rate estimation.

Beer-Lambert Law

The principle that light absorption is related to concentration and path length, used to estimate oxygen saturation from two wavelengths.

Oxyhemoglobin (HbO2)

Hemoglobin bound to oxygen. It absorbs red and infrared light differently than deoxyhemoglobin, enabling SpO2 calculation.

Deoxyhemoglobin (Hb)

Hemoglobin not bound to oxygen. Its higher absorption of red light helps determine oxygen saturation differences.

AC and DC Components

AC is the pulsatile component from arterial blood flow; DC is the baseline from tissue and non-pulsatile blood. Their ratio is clinically important.

Ratio of Ratios (R)

The computed ratio (AC/DC at red) divided by (AC/DC at infrared), converted by calibration curves into SpO2 estimates.

Hypoxemia

Low oxygen level in arterial blood. Sustained SpO2 below normal ranges can indicate mild, moderate, or severe hypoxemia.

Tachycardia / Bradycardia

Tachycardia is elevated heart rate and bradycardia is reduced heart rate, each interpreted relative to patient age and clinical context.

Motion Artifact

False signal changes caused by movement, which can degrade waveform quality and produce inaccurate SpO2 or pulse readings.

Signal Quality

A confidence indicator based on waveform stability and noise level. Better signal quality leads to more reliable measurements.

Calibration Curve

A manufacturer-derived mapping from optical ratios to oxygen saturation percentages based on validated experimental references.

Peripheral Circulation

Blood flow in extremities such as fingers and toes. Poor peripheral perfusion can reduce pulse oximeter performance.

Nail Polish Interference

Dark or opaque nail polish may alter transmitted light and potentially distort measurements in some sensor configurations.

Clinical Alarm Threshold

Configured limits for SpO2 and pulse that trigger warnings when readings exceed safe boundaries for monitoring protocols.

Ambient Light Interference

External light from sunlight or strong lamps that can enter the sensor and introduce optical noise into the measured signal.

Cyanosis

Bluish discoloration of skin or mucosa caused by reduced oxygenation, often a late clinical sign rather than an early warning.

Dyshemoglobinemia

Abnormal hemoglobin species such as carboxyhemoglobin or methemoglobin that can distort pulse oximetry estimates.

Pleth Variability Index (PVI)

A derived metric from PPG waveform variation over respiration, sometimes used as a rough indicator of fluid responsiveness.

Pulse Deficit

A mismatch between electrical heart activity and effective peripheral pulse, which may reduce pulse-detected beats at the finger.

Sensor Site Selection

Choosing an appropriate body site, usually fingertip in this lab, where perfusion is adequate and motion is minimized.

Signal Dropout

Temporary loss of a usable pulsatile waveform due to motion, poor attachment, low perfusion, or optical obstruction.

Trending vs Spot Reading

Single values can fluctuate; trend interpretation over time is generally more meaningful than isolated spot readings.
